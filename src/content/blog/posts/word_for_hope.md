# A Word for Hope

## The Great Challenge for Africa as a way to not lose hope

*Originally published on Substack on April 28, 2023. This post is cross-posted from [Substack](https://antoniok.substack.com/p/a-word-for-hope).*

The recent history of African people has been that of mockery and suffering. This is not out of the ordinary. It mirrors the classic capitalistic tale: "The rich will get richer and the poor will stay poor," on a bigger scale. We have always been the poor ones, the underdeveloped, the underprivileged, almost by design.[^1] Always used as the quintessential nonexample, our continent of birth seems to be our greatest curse.

We are "the shithole people,"[^2] from which our brothers in the North want to distance themselves.[^3] It is easy to feel upset by those realities and lose hope. But we shouldn't.

The Great Challenge is an expression that I've dubbed for a change in mindset that I think we Africans crucially need if we want to escape the bottomless pit of economic alienation. The claim rests on 2 main points.

## 1. An Artificially Intelligent World: A crucial era to Redefine Africa's Future

Nations that leverage technological revolutions can secure themselves countless opportunities and get even more advanced. Africa has missed several of those booms and we are struggling to catch up. Several African economies still rely on subsistence farming to support the majority of their population, similar to China's state before the 1950s.[^4] And like China, our attempts for development will have to encompass a long industrialization process and a tough technological catch-up game.

For us, technological revolutions are double-edged swords: a blessing of an opportunity to lift ourselves out of poverty and a promise that, if missed, we'll sink even further, and it will be even harder to escape. The current revolution, that of machine intelligence, will be transformative.[^5] Because of this, we probably live in one of the most important centuries of human history, and the weight on the shoulders of Africans is the heaviest it has ever been.

All around, we can hear whispering voices: "Will you make it?"

## 2. The Great Challenge: Alternative Framework for a Counterproductive Mentality

No one knows the answer to this question, but an unfortunate narrative is predominant: Africa's worst faces get the most press wherever we look, and quite sadly, from Africans themselves.[^6] Have we decided to whisper back that we can't do it? We are the perpetrators of an attention bias which, like in depression, exacerbates our situation as in a self-fulfilling prophecy.

Hutchinson's wisdom resonates in my heart:

> Words are how others define us, but we can define ourselves any way we choose.

I have decided to define all the factors stacked up against us as a challenge for me personally and for my continent. A challenge from Trump, from the world, from the future. And when anybody thinks that we can't make it, I decide to not whine or feel hurt, but we just answer with pride, "Alright, bet! We'll show you." Because this is the Great Challenge, the greatest of our lives as 21st-century Africans living through an objectively weird era.

When things go sideways, we should remember that the deeper we have fallen, the greater the rise will be. We should define ourselves not as the sum of our pitfalls, but as the immense opportunity we have to rise, against all odds. And together, we can rise, for Africa.
